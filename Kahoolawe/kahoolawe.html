Military use

During World War II, Kahoolawe was used as a bombing range by the U.S. Military. In May 1941, the U.S. Army leased a section of the island for military training for just $1.00 a year. Following the attack on Pearl Harbor, the military took control of the entire island. The island served as a training ground for combined arms operations, involving ships, aircraft, and ground troops. Kahoolawes military use continued until 1990, after which efforts began to restore the island and preserve its cultural significance.

Kahoolawe Island Reserve

Kahoolawe Island Reserve, located in Hawaii, is a sacred and culturally significant site for Native Hawaiians. Formerly used as a bombing range by the U.S. military, the island is now managed by the Kahoʻolawe Island Reserve Commission with a focus on restoration and preservation. Access to the island is restricted, primarily reserved for native Hawaiian cultural, spiritual, and subsistence purposes. Volunteers can participate in restoration efforts through organized work trips, contributing to the reestablishment of native flora and the removal of invasive species. The reserve encompasses several named peaks, with Pu’u Moa’ulanui being the highest, reflecting the islands unique ecological and geological features.

Lua Makika

Lua Makika is the highest point on Kahoolawe, an island in Hawaii, reaching approximately 1,477 feet (450 meters) above sea level. It is the crater at the summit of Puu Moaulanui, part of an extinct shield volcano that formed during the Pleistocene epoch. Kahoolawe, where Lua Makika is located, is the smallest of the eight main volcanic islands in the Hawaiian Islands and has no permanent residents. The islands low elevation and position in the rain shadow of Mauis Haleakalā Volcano result in a dry climate with less than 26 inches of annual rainfall. Historically used for military training and bombing practice, Kahoolawe is now designated for native Hawaiian cultural, spiritual, and subsistence purposes only.

Environment on Kahoolawe

Historically, Kahoolawe was a dry forest with intermittent streams, but human activities and overgrazing transformed it into a savanna. The island is notoriously dry, recieving less than 26 inches of rain anually, partly due to its position in the rain shadow of Mauis Haleakala volcano. Ecological restoration efforts are underway, focusing on controlling erosion and replanting native species to combat the severe degradation from past military bombings and overgrazing. Innovative conservation technology using AI-equipped camera traps and drones is being tested to tackle invasive species, like feral cats, that threaten native seabird populations. The ongoing conservation efforts on Kahoolawe highlight a proactive approach to restoring its delicate ecosystem, emphasizing the importance of controlling erosion, replanting native species, and combatting invasive threats like feral cats to preserve the island's natural heritage for future generations.

Cultural Significance Importance

Kahoolawe holds a profound cultural significance for Native Hawaiians as a sacred place for celestial navigation, a vital fishing ground, and a site for cultural and religious rituals. It was historically used for celestial navigation training, agriculture, and spiritual practices, reflecting the deep connection between the Hawaiian people and their land. The island became a symbol of resistance and cultural renaissance when the Protect Kahoolawe Ohana (PKO) led protests against military bombings, which ceased in 1990. Kahoolawe is at the heart of the Hawaiian Island chain, with specific locations like Kealaikahiki and Moaulaiki being crucial for traditional navigation and understanding wind patterns and stars. Today, efforts are ongoing to restore the islands ecosystem and preserve its cultural heritage, making it a testament to resilience and the importance of safeguarding sacred spaces
