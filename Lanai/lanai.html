Polihua

Polihua Beach, located on the north shore of Lānai, is a secluded destination ideal for sunbathing and beachcombing. The journey to this remote two-mile stretch of pristine coastline requires a 4-wheel drive or a tour guide, as its about an hours drive from Lānai City. While swimming is not recommended due to strong winds and currents, the beach offers a tranquil atmosphere and stunning views, especially between December and April when migrating humpback whales can be spotted offshore. There are no restroom facilities, and visitors are encouraged to check ocean conditions at HIOCEANSAFETY.COM before heading out. Despite the challenging access, many find the serene environment and the chance to possibly have the beach to themselves well worth the effort.

Hulopoe

Hulopoe Bay, located on the southern coast of Lanai, is renowned for its pearl-white sand and crystal blue waters, making it a prime spot for snorkeling and swimming. The bay is home to protected tide pools teeming with marine life, offering a peaceful spot for exploration and appreciation of natures diversity. Visitors can often spot dolphins in the bay, and during the winter months, humpback whales may be spotted in the waters. The beach park provides comfort like picnic tables, barbecue grills, restrooms, and showers, enhancing the comfort of a day spent by the ocean. For those interested in local legends, a short hike along the cliffs reveals Puu Pehe, also known as Sweetheart Rock, steeped in the lore of a heartbroken warrior.

Kaunolu

Kaunolu, located on the southern tip of Lānai, Hawaii, is a historic gem that was once a favored fishing retreat for King Kamehameha I. Today, it stands as the largest surviving ruins of a prehistoric Hawaiian village, offering a glimpse into ancient Hawaiian culture. Visitors can explore the sites sacred temple remains, petroglyphs, and the famed “Kahekilis Leap,” a 60-foot cliff dive site. Accessible by a four-wheel drive, the journey to Kaunolu promises a spectacular view of Lānais southern sea cliffs and a profound connection to Hawaiis past. As a sacred place, tourists are reminded to approach Kaunolu with respect and reverence for its historical significance.

Koloiki

Koloiki Ridge Trail offers a four-hour hike through diverse terrains, having stunning views. The trail is a 4.5-mile round trip, marked by red signs, guiding hikers through rainforests, pine forests, and a lava field. Its known for its end point, which provides a panoramic view of Maui and Molokai across the Maunalei Valley. Koloiki, meaning “little crawler,” refers to the narrow ridge leading towards the ocean, separating Maunalei Valley and Naio Gulch. The trails history is rich, once used for harvesting rain for taro fields and later supplying water to the Dole pineapple plantation.

Keahiakawelo

Keahiakawelo, also known as the Garden of the Gods, is a breathtaking rock garden located on the northwest side of Lānai, Hawaii. This mystical site features a unique lunar landscape with boulders and rock towers formed by centuries of erosion. According to Hawaiian legend, the areas barren appearance is the result of a contest between two priests to keep a fire burning longer than the other. The colors of the rocks change depending on the light, displaying beautiful shades from orange to red to brown, especially captivating at dusk. Accessible only by four-wheel drive or mountain bike, its a place of great spiritual significance and offers panoramic views of neighboring islands on clear days.
