Papohaku

Pāpōhaku Beach, also known as Three Mile Beach, is a stunning expanse of white sand stretching three miles along Molokais west end. Its one of Hawaiis largest beaches, offering plenty of space for relaxation without the crowds. Visitors can enjoy camping, picnicking, and breathtaking views of Oahu across the Kaiwi Channel. While swimming is possible when conditions are calm, caution is advised due to strong currents, especially during the winter months. Pāpōhaku is a serene getaway for those seeking a more secluded and pristine beach experience in Hawaii.

Kamakou

Kamakou is a hidden gem for nature enthusiasts, offering a pristine environment that feels almost untouched by time. The preserve is home to over 250 rare Hawaiian plants, with 219 species found nowhere else in the world, making it a unique botanical haven. Birdwatchers can delight in the songs of the olomao and kākāwahie, two bird species nearing extinction, adding to the area’s ecological significance. Adventurous visitors can traverse the 3-mile round-trip boardwalk through the lush rainforest, immersing themselves in the serene beauty of the landscape. However, access requires a four-wheel drive vehicle and preparation for rugged terrain, ensuring that the journey to Kamakou is as memorable as the destination itself.

Kalaupapa

Kalaupapa is a place of profound historical significance and natural beauty. Established as a National Historical Park, it commemorates the history of individuals with Hansens disease (leprosy) who were isolated there. Tourism to Kalaupapa is highly regulated; visitors must be at least 16 years old and obtain a permit through a guided tour, although tours are currently suspended. The park offers no medical facilities, dining, or shopping, and photography of residents and their properties is strictly prohibited without permission. Despite these restrictions, the serene landscape and the powerful stories of Kalaupapas past make it a unique and impressive destination for those who can visit.

Kawakiu

Kawakiu Beach, located on Molokai, Hawaii, is a secluded paradise for those seeking tranquility away from crowded spots. Known for its white sand and turquoise waters, its a hidden gem partially sheltered from the winds, making it ideal for swimming when the seas are calm. The beach can be accessed via a 45-minute to 1-hour hike along the coast from Make Horse Beach, offering a rewarding adventure for nature enthusiasts. Visitors often find themselves in almost guaranteed solitude, with the chance to experience the beachs serene beauty in peace1. Whether its for hiking, swimming, or simply relaxing, Kawakiu Beach is a must-visit for those looking to connect with Hawaiis natural brilliance.

Halawa Valley

Halawa Valley, located on Molokais East End, is a lush and historic Hawaiian valley with a rich cultural heritage dating back to around 650 AD. Its known for its stunning natural beauty, including towering waterfalls like the 250-foot Mooula Falls and the 500-foot Hipuapua Falls. Guided hikes are available, offering a unique cultural experience and the chance to learn about the valleys history from local guides. The journey to Halawa Valley is an adventure in itself, passing by Hawaiian Fishponds, beautiful beaches, and the first Christian church built on Molokai in 1833. Visitors can enjoy activities such as swimming, hiking, and embracing the aloha spirit in this serene and picturesque location
